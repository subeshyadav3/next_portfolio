## Introduction

Cleanliness is often said to be next to godliness, and there is a good reason for that saying.

<Quote author="Mahatma Gandhi">
Sanitation is more important than independence.
</Quote>

A clean body, a clean home, and a clean environment are essential for a healthy and happy life. Yet cleanliness is something many of us take for granted. We do not think about it until we get sick, and then we wish we had been more careful.

Hygiene is the practice of keeping ourselves and our surroundings clean to prevent disease and promote health. It includes everything from washing our hands before meals to keeping our streets free of garbage. In a country like Nepal, where many diseases are preventable through basic hygiene practices, understanding and practicing cleanliness is not just important — it is a matter of life and death.

## Personal Hygiene: The First Line of Defense

Personal hygiene starts with the basics. Washing your hands with soap and water after using the toilet, before eating, and after coming home from outside. This single habit can prevent diseases like diarrhea, cholera, and typhoid, which are still common in many parts of Nepal. During the COVID-19 pandemic, we all learned just how important hand washing is. It is one of the simplest and most effective ways to protect ourselves and others.

Bathing regularly, brushing your teeth twice a day, wearing clean clothes, and keeping your hair and nails clean are all part of personal hygiene. These habits not only keep you healthy but also make you feel good about yourself. When you are clean and well-groomed, you feel more confident and comfortable around others.

For students, personal hygiene is especially important. You spend a lot of time in close contact with classmates, sharing desks, books, and sometimes even food. If one student is sick, it can quickly spread to others. Good hygiene habits protect not just you but everyone around you.

## Environmental Cleanliness: Our Shared Responsibility

Cleanliness is not just about our own bodies. It is also about the spaces we live in. A clean home, a clean school, and a clean community contribute to our physical and mental well-being. When our environment is clean, we feel more peaceful and productive. When it is dirty, we feel stressed and uncomfortable.

Unfortunately, littering is still a common problem in Nepal. You can see plastic bottles, food wrappers, and other garbage thrown along roadsides, in rivers, and in public spaces. This is not just ugly to look at — it is dangerous. Garbage attracts rats, mosquitoes, and other pests that spread diseases. Plastic waste clogs our drainage systems and causes flooding during the monsoon. And when garbage is burned, it releases toxic fumes that harm our health.

The solution starts with each one of us. Do not throw garbage on the ground. Use a dustbin. If there is no dustbin nearby, hold onto your trash until you find one. Separate your waste into biodegradable and non-biodegradable. Compost your kitchen waste. Recycle what you can. These small actions, when practiced by millions of people, make a huge difference.

Schools can play a big role in promoting cleanliness. Regular cleaning schedules, proper waste management systems, and awareness campaigns can create a culture of cleanliness among students. When students learn these habits at school, they take them home and share them with their families.

## Cleanliness and Disease Prevention

The connection between cleanliness and health is well established. Many of the most common diseases in Nepal — diarrhea, respiratory infections, skin diseases, and vector-borne diseases like dengue and malaria — can be prevented through better hygiene and sanitation.

According to the World Health Organization, proper hand washing with soap can reduce the risk of diarrheal diseases by up to 48 percent. Access to clean drinking water and proper sanitation facilities can prevent a wide range of waterborne diseases. Yet millions of people in Nepal still lack access to these basic necessities.

Improving hygiene and sanitation in Nepal requires effort from the government, communities, and individuals. The government must invest in clean water supply, sewage systems, and waste management infrastructure. Communities must work together to keep their neighborhoods clean. And individuals must practice good hygiene habits every day.

## The Role of Students in Promoting Cleanliness

Students can be powerful agents of change when it comes to cleanliness. You can start by keeping your own surroundings clean — your room, your desk, your school compound. You can participate in cleanliness drives organized by your school or community. You can educate your family members and neighbors about the importance of hygiene. You can lead by example.

Many schools in Nepal have student-led clubs that focus on environmental awareness and cleanliness. These clubs organize activities like cleaning campaigns, tree planting, and waste management projects. Being part of such a club is a great way to make a positive impact while developing leadership and teamwork skills.

Remember that cleanliness is not just about avoiding disease. It is about respect — respect for yourself, respect for others, and respect for the environment. When you keep yourself and your surroundings clean, you show that you value your health and the well-being of those around you.

## Conclusion

Cleanliness and hygiene are the foundation of good health. They are simple, affordable, and effective ways to prevent disease and improve quality of life. In Nepal, where preventable diseases still claim too many lives, practicing good hygiene is not just a personal choice — it is a social responsibility.

As students, we have the knowledge and the energy to make a difference. Let us start with ourselves. Let us keep our bodies clean, our homes tidy, and our communities beautiful. Let us be the generation that says no to littering and yes to cleanliness. Because a clean Nepal is a healthy Nepal, and a healthy Nepal is a prosperous Nepal. The change begins with each one of us, starting today.
